TOPIC 5 — Overfitting vs Underfitting

 What are Overfitting and Underfitting?

When we train a Machine Learning or Deep Learning model, we want it to learn patterns from the data—not simply memorize it or fail to learn.

There are three possible situations during training:

Underfitting ❌
Overfitting ❌
Good Fit (Balanced Model) ✅

The goal of every AI model is to achieve a Good Fit.

 Real-World Example

Imagine three students preparing for an exam.

Student A (Underfitting)

Studies very little.

Knows only a few concepts.

Performs poorly in both practice tests and the final exam.

Student B (Overfitting)

Memorizes every practice question.

Scores very high in practice tests.

Fails when new questions appear in the real exam.

Student C (Good Fit)

Understands the concepts instead of memorizing.

Performs well in practice tests and the final exam.

This is exactly how machine learning models behave.

 What is Underfitting?

Underfitting happens when a model is too simple and cannot learn the important patterns in the training data.

It performs poorly on both:

Training Data
Testing Data
Characteristics of Underfitting
Low training accuracy
Low testing accuracy
High training error
High testing error

The model has not learned enough.

Example

Training Accuracy = 65%

Testing Accuracy = 63%

Both values are low.

This means the model has not learned the relationship properly.

Real-World Example

Imagine a student who studies only one chapter for an exam containing ten chapters.

They perform poorly in both:

Practice tests
Final exam

The student simply did not learn enough.

 Causes of Underfitting

Underfitting usually occurs because:

The model is too simple.
The model has very few neurons or layers.
Training stops too early.
Too few epochs are used.
Important features are missing.
 Solutions for Underfitting

To improve an underfitted model:

Increase the number of hidden layers.
Add more neurons.
Train for more epochs.
Use a more powerful model.
Improve the quality of input features.

 What is Overfitting?

Overfitting happens when the model memorizes the training data instead of learning general patterns.

It performs extremely well on training data but poorly on new, unseen data.

Characteristics of Overfitting
Very high training accuracy
Lower testing accuracy
Low training error
High testing error

The model remembers the training data but cannot generalize.

Example

Training Accuracy = 99%

Testing Accuracy = 70%

The large difference shows that the model has overfitted.

Real-World Example

Imagine a student memorizing all previous exam papers.

If the final exam contains different questions, the student struggles.

The student memorized instead of understanding the concepts.

 Causes of Overfitting:

Overfitting can happen when:

The model is too large.
Too many neurons are used.
Too many hidden layers are added.
Training continues for too many epochs.
The dataset is too small.
No regularization techniques are used.
🔧 Solutions for Overfitting

The most common solutions are:

1. Dropout

Randomly turns off some neurons during training.

This prevents the model from depending too much on specific neurons.

2. L1 / L2 Regularization

Adds a penalty when weights become too large.

This encourages the model to learn simpler patterns.

3. More Training Data

More examples help the model learn general patterns instead of memorizing.

4. Smaller Model

Reducing the number of neurons or layers makes memorization less likely.

5. Early Stopping

Stops training automatically when validation performance no longer improves.

This prevents unnecessary learning and reduces overfitting.

 What is a Good Fit?

A Good Fit means the model has learned the important patterns and performs well on both training and testing data.

Training and testing accuracy should be high and close to each other.

Example

Training Accuracy = 92%

Testing Accuracy = 90%

The difference is very small.

This indicates that the model has generalized well.

Real-World Example

Imagine a student who understands every topic.

They perform well in:

Practice tests
Surprise quizzes
Final exam

This is the ideal situation.

📊 Comparing the Three Cases:

Model Type	Training Accuracy	Testing Accuracy	Result
Underfitting	Low	Low	Model did not learn enough
Good Fit	High	High (Close to Training)	Best model
Overfitting	Very High	Much Lower	Model memorized training data

📉 Training vs Validation Accuracy:

While training a neural network, we usually monitor:

Training Accuracy
Validation Accuracy

These values help identify whether the model is underfitting or overfitting.

Underfitting Graph

Training Accuracy → Low

Validation Accuracy → Low

Both improve very slowly.

Good Fit Graph

Training Accuracy → High

Validation Accuracy → Almost the same

The two curves stay close together.

Overfitting Graph

Training Accuracy → Keeps increasing.

Validation Accuracy → Stops improving or starts decreasing.

The gap between the two curves becomes large.

 What is the Accuracy Gap?

The Accuracy Gap is the difference between:

Training Accuracy

and

Validation (or Test) Accuracy.

Small Gap

Example:

Training = 92%

Validation = 90%

Gap = 2%

This is a healthy model.

Large Gap

Example:

Training = 99%

Validation = 72%

Gap = 27%

This indicates overfitting.

 Techniques Used in This Project:

The "Good Model" uses two important techniques.

1. Dropout

Dropout randomly switches off some neurons during training.

Benefits:

Prevents memorization
Improves generalization
Reduces overfitting
Real-World Example

Imagine solving a group project.

If one student is absent, the others still complete the work.

Similarly, Dropout forces all neurons to learn instead of relying on a few.

2. L2 Regularization

L2 Regularization adds a small penalty for very large weights.

Benefits:

Keeps the model simple
Prevents overly complex solutions
Reduces overfitting
Real-World Example

Imagine a teacher deducting marks for writing unnecessary details.

Students learn to write only the important points.

L2 Regularization encourages the neural network to learn only the most useful patterns.

 Why is Generalization Important?

The ultimate goal of any AI model is generalization.

Generalization means the model performs well on data it has never seen before.

A model that memorizes training data is not useful in real-world applications.

Real-World Example

A face recognition system should recognize new faces, not only the images used during training.

 Quick Summary:

Underfitting occurs when the model is too simple and fails to learn the important patterns in the data.
Underfitted models have low training accuracy and low testing accuracy.
Overfitting occurs when the model memorizes the training data instead of learning general patterns.
Overfitted models have very high training accuracy but much lower testing accuracy.
A Good Fit means the model performs well on both training and testing data, with only a small difference between their accuracies.
The Accuracy Gap (difference between training and validation accuracy) helps identify overfitting.
Common techniques to reduce overfitting include:
Dropout
L1/L2 Regularization
Early Stopping
Using More Training Data
Building a Smaller Model
The ultimate goal of every machine learning model is generalization, which means making accurate predictions on new, unseen data.

📝 Overfitting vs Underfitting Cheat Sheet:

Concept	Underfitting	Good Fit	Overfitting
Learning	Too Little	Balanced	Too Much (Memorized)
Training Accuracy	Low	High	Very High
Testing Accuracy	Low	High	Lower than Training
Accuracy Gap	Small (but low performance)	Small	Large
Performance on New Data	Poor	Excellent	Poor
Main Cause	Model too simple	Proper model	Model too complex
Solution	Increase model capacity, train longer	Maintain current settings	Dropout, Regularization, Early Stopping, More Data

One-Line Concept:

Underfitting means the model learns too little, Overfitting means it learns too much by memorizing the data, and a Good Fit means it learns the right patterns and performs well on both training and unseen data.
