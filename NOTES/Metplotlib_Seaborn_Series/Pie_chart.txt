📘 Topic 5 — Pie Chart (Percentage Distribution)

What is a Pie Chart?

A Pie Chart is a circular graph that shows how each category contributes to the whole using slices (or sectors).

In simple words,

A Pie Chart is used to display percentages or proportions of different categories in a dataset.

Each slice of the pie represents one category, and the size of the slice depends on its percentage of the total.

Definition

A Pie Chart is a circular statistical graph that divides a whole dataset into slices, where each slice represents the percentage or proportion of a category.

Why Do We Use a Pie Chart?

A Pie Chart helps answer questions like:

Which category has the largest share?
Which category has the smallest share?
What percentage does each category represent?
How is the total divided among different categories?

Instead of reading numbers, we can quickly understand the percentage distribution.

What Does a Pie Chart Show?

A Pie Chart shows:

Percentage distribution.
Proportion of each category.
Part-to-whole relationship.
Largest and smallest categories.
Components of a Pie Chart

A professional Pie Chart usually contains:

Circular chart
Slices (sectors)
Labels
Percentages
Colors
Title
Legend (optional)
Exploded slices (optional)
Shadow effect (optional)
What is a Slice?

A slice is one section of the Pie Chart.

Each slice represents one category and its percentage of the total.

Larger percentage → Larger slice.

Smaller percentage → Smaller slice.

What is Percentage Distribution?

Percentage distribution tells us what portion of the total belongs to each category.

For example:

If there are 100 students:

A+ = 15 students = 15%
A = 25 students = 25%
B = 30 students = 30%
C = 20 students = 20%
Fail = 10 students = 10%

A Pie Chart displays these percentages as slices of a circle.

What is an Exploded Slice?

An Exploded Slice is a slice that is slightly separated from the rest of the Pie Chart.

It is used to highlight important categories.

For example:

Highest sales product
Best-performing department
Lowest-performing category
Failed students

This makes the highlighted category more noticeable.

Why Use Different Colors?

Different colors help to:

Distinguish categories.
Improve readability.
Make the chart attractive.
Highlight important information.
Why Show Percentages?

Displaying percentages allows viewers to immediately understand:

The share of each category.
The contribution of each section.
Which category dominates the dataset.

🌍 Real-World Example 1 — Student Grades

A teacher wants to show grade distribution.

Grade	Students
A+	15
A	25
B	30
C	20
Fail	10

Instead of showing a table, the teacher creates a Pie Chart.

The chart instantly shows:

Most students received Grade B.
Very few students failed.
A and B grades make up the largest portion.

Managers can quickly identify the largest expenses.

Why is Pie Chart Important in AI & Data Science?

Although Pie Charts are less common in Machine Learning, they are useful for:

Showing class distribution.
Visualizing survey responses.
Displaying category percentages.
Presenting business reports.
Summarizing categorical data.

They are commonly used in dashboards and presentations.

Advantages of Pie Charts
Easy to understand.
Clearly shows percentages.
Attractive visual presentation.
Highlights the largest and smallest categories.
Best for showing part-to-whole relationships.
Ideal for reports and presentations.
Limitations of Pie Charts
Not suitable for many categories.
Difficult to compare very similar slice sizes.
Only works well for percentage-based data.
Less effective for large datasets.
When Should You Use a Pie Chart?

Use a Pie Chart when you want to show:

Grade distribution
Market share
Budget allocation
Survey results
Department expenses
Product category percentages
Customer demographics
Election vote percentages

Whenever your goal is to show how a whole is divided into parts, use a Pie Chart.

Pie Chart vs Bar Chart vs Histogram vs Scatter Plot
Pie Chart	Bar Chart	Histogram	Scatter Plot
Shows percentages	Compares categories	Shows data distribution	Shows relationship between variables
Circular graph	Uses bars	Uses touching bars	Uses dots
Best for part-to-whole	Best for comparison	Best for numerical distribution	Best for correlation
Percentage-based	Category-based	Range-based	Relationship-based
